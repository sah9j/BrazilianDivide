Brazil As a Country

Brazil is the largest country within South America, covering roughly half of the continent, and the seventh most populated country in the world with 214 million inhabitants. Its capital of Brasilia is nestled within the center of the country; it was founded in 1960 and boasts a population of roughly 4.8 million- the third largest city within Brazil. The country is also well known for the coffee it produces, exporting the most amount of coffee compared to any other nation; Brazil accounts for a whopping 40% of the world’s coffee trade. The country’s favorite sport is football (for the American audience, soccer), with some of the best football players hailing from Brazil. Each year, Brazil celebrates its Independence Day on September 7th, marking where the country declared its independence from Portugal in 1822.


Brazil's History

Before being founded in 1822, the region was home to a diverse set of tribes. These tribes, however, were soon pushed out of their lands by the Portuguese, and by the mid 1900’s suffered an 80% decline in their population. Brazil was officially founded when Pedro I declared independence from Portugal and formed the Empire of Brazil. As time progressed, Brazil became a republic. Multiple revolutions occurred starting in the 1920’s to overthrow the corrupt republic government, becoming a fully democratic by the early 1980’s. Today, Brazil still democratically elects its leaders, though civil unrest is still prominent during these elections.


Brazil's Government

The government of Brazil is a federal presidential constitutional republic, which makes use of representative democracy to fulfill the country’s needs. The government itself is broken into three main branches- executive, legislative, and judicial. The executive branch is comprised of the president, along with their cabinet members. The legislative branch is run by the “Congresso Nacional”, or the National Congress. The final branch, judicial, is made up of the multitude of courts found in each state, alongside the federal systems.
